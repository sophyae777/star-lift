Project Overview

What it is, why we built it, and how it works

The S.T.A.R. Lift — Self Transfer Automated Raising Lift — is a retrofit attachment for standard wheelchairs that enables users to independently transfer themselves from their wheelchair to a bed, and back again, without the assistance of a caregiver. By simply clamping onto an existing wheelchair, the device restores a fundamental sense of independence to users who would otherwise rely entirely on another person for one of the most routine parts of their day.

Living in New York City, our team constantly noticed the gap between the city's promises of accessibility and the reality on the ground — from inaccessible subway stations to spaces that simply weren't designed with wheelchair users in mind. When we dove deeper into the research, we found that bed-to-chair transfers were one of the most overlooked problems in this space. They happen multiple times a day, every day, and they take a serious toll — both on caregivers, who suffer high rates of musculoskeletal injuries from repeated manual lifting, and on users, whose independence and dignity are affected every time they need to ask for help. We built the S.T.A.R. Lift to change that.

Using the S.T.A.R. Lift is straightforward. The device clamps directly onto the user's wheelchair — no heavy installation required. Once positioned next to the bed, the user raises their legs to a horizontal position using the leg raising panel, controlled by a handheld remote. Next, they press a button to activate the scissor lift, which raises them to the height of the bed. The linear actuator then slides them laterally over the bed surface. Throughout the entire process, fully adjustable arm supports provide stability and upper body support, allowing the user to shift themselves safely onto the bed. Every step is designed to be repeatable, predictable, and entirely in the user's control.

Leg Raising Panel

The leg raising panel rotates the user's legs up 180 degrees to a position parallel with the bed surface. It is electrically actuated using a servo motor and controlled via handheld remote.

Sliding Mechanism

The linear actuator slides the user laterally from the wheelchair over to the bed surface. Controlled via remote, this mechanism handles the full lateral transfer smoothly and safely.

Arm Support

The arm supports are the only mechanically actuated component of the device. Fully adjustable in height, length, and position, one side swings out to assist the user in shifting onto the bed. They lock securely in any position and provide stability throughout the entire transfer.

Literature & Prior Art

Research, motivation, and what makes us different

In the U.S., about 5.5 million people rely on a wheelchair for mobility, and a significant portion require assisted transfers multiple times a day. Research shows that providing transfer assistance has caused work-related injuries in more than 78% of physical therapists and their assistants at rehabilitation facilities. Beyond caregiver injuries, the lack of independence experienced by users during transfers affects their dignity and quality of life daily. Living in New York City, our team saw this gap firsthand — and set out to design a solution that puts control back in the hands of the user.

Transfer Boards

Transfer boards allow users to slide between surfaces, but they require significant trunk control, balance, and upper body strength. This limits their accessibility and makes them unreliable for users with more severe mobility impairments. The S.T.A.R. Lift requires none of these — the mechanisms do the work.

Hoists & Slings

Hoists lift users via a sling system but require a caregiver to operate them at all times, doing nothing to restore user independence. They are also slow, cumbersome, and can feel undignified for users. The S.T.A.R. Lift is designed for fully independent operation with a simple, repeatable sequence.

Ceiling & Floor Lift Systems

Powered lift systems can assist with transfers but require heavy, permanent installation that is not feasible for most home or care environments. The S.T.A.R. Lift requires no installation — it simply clamps onto any standard wheelchair and is ready to use immediately.

Analysis

How our analyses shaped key design decisions

To validate the structural integrity and stability of the S.T.A.R. Lift, our team performed four analyses targeting the most critical design constraints — stability, structural strength, and safe load transfer across each major mechanism.

Image / Video of Study Here

Analysis 1

Center of Mass Analysis

Question: Will the S.T.A.R. Lift remain stable throughout every stage of the transfer, or is there a risk of tipping?

Method: We tracked the system's center of mass in SolidWorks through all four transfer states — initial position, leg panel raised, scissor lift extended, and fully laterally extended — across a range of user masses up to 118.8 kg.

Result: The device showed no risk of tipping in any direction across all tested configurations. This confirmed our 20 kg design target is safe, and gave us confidence the device holds up even beyond that.

Image / Video of Study Here

Analysis 2

Scissor Lift Analysis

Question: Can the scissor lift links handle the forces placed on them during the hardest part of the lift — coming up from a fully collapsed position?

Method: We ran a finite element analysis (FEA) on a single scissor lift link under a conservative axial force of 2,401 N, and verified our results through mesh convergence up to 469,520 elements.

Result: The maximum stress was 82.51 MPa with a factor of safety of 3.4, confirming the links are strong enough to handle the worst-case loading scenario.

Image / Video of Study Here

Analysis 3

Sliding Analysis

Question: When the seat is fully extended over the bed, can the sliding panel support the user's weight without excessive bending or deflection?

Method: We performed FEA on the sliding top adaptor at its most vulnerable position — fully extended — using a conservative load of 56.1 kg spread across the seat surface. Multiple mesh convergence studies were run to ensure accuracy.

Result: Maximum stress came in at 7.82 MPa with a factor of safety of 35.3 and a maximum deflection of just 0.14 mm, well within acceptable limits for safe use.

Image / Video of Study Here

Analysis 4

Clamp Analysis

Question: Are the clamp brackets that connect the scissor lift to the wheelchair strong enough to handle the full load of the device and user?

Method: We ran FEA on a single aluminum (Al 6061) clamp bracket, applying a downward force of 199.09 N at the mounting surface and performing mesh convergence up to 68,073 elements.

Result: The bracket showed a maximum stress of 8.33 MPa and a factor of safety of 33.1, with virtually no displacement. The clamps are more than capable of handling the required loads.
